Two datasets, kept separate on purpose

No single public dataset has cholesterol, blood pressure, smoking, BMI, and glucose as real, trainable features all at once. Rather than force one blended model that quietly mixes a diagnostic test result with a self-reported survey answer, this project trains and reports two separate models, each scoped to what its data can actually support.

UCI Heart Disease (clinical model)

918 patients after cleaning, combining data from Cleveland, Hungary, Switzerland, and the VA in Long Beach. It predicts angiographically-confirmed coronary artery disease using chest pain type, resting ECG, exercise stress test results, fluoroscopy vessel counts, and thallium test results: real clinical test data from patients already being evaluated at a hospital.

CDC BRFSS 2015 (lifestyle model)

253,680 people who answered the CDC's Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System survey. It predicts whether someone has ever been told they have heart disease or had a heart attack, based on smoking, BMI, diet, physical activity, alcohol use, and demographics: everyday survey answers, not lab results.

Pooling them would mean treating a diagnostic test result and a self-reported survey answer as if they were the same kind of data, even though they predict different outcomes in different populations. So they stay separate, which is why the calculator has two clearly labeled tabs instead of one blended one.

Headline results

Both models land right in line with the published benchmarks for each dataset.

Both land around an AUROC of 0.88, matching the widely cited benchmark (0.886) for this dataset. The strongest SHAP drivers were asymptomatic chest pain, ST depression, sex, cholesterol, exercise-induced angina, max heart rate, thallium results, age, and vessels seen on fluoroscopy: all established cardiac risk factors.

An AUROC of about 0.85 matches the published benchmark (0.848). Both models correct for the dataset's roughly 90/10 class imbalance. By SHAP importance, the strongest predictors are age, self-rated general health, high blood pressure, sex, and high cholesterol, all clinical or demographic factors. Lifestyle factors (smoking, diet, activity, alcohol, BMI) only account for about 7% of the total SHAP-explained importance, with smoking the strongest single lifestyle factor.
