AI-powered early heart disease risk prediction, built and evaluated honestly.

MedMind trains and rigorously evaluates heart disease risk models on two independent public datasets, then puts them behind a two-tab "what-if" simulator: enter a patient or respondent profile, drag sliders on the modifiable risk factors, and see the predicted risk update live along with a SHAP explanation of why.

Two datasets, kept deliberately separate

No single public dataset has cholesterol, blood pressure, smoking, BMI, and glucose all as real, trainable features — so rather than force a blended model that would silently mix a diagnostic test result with a self-reported survey answer, this project trains and reports two honestly-scoped models.

🩺 UCI Heart Disease (clinical model)

918 rows after cleaning, combining Cleveland, Hungarian, Swiss, and VA Long Beach cohorts. Predicts angiographically-confirmed coronary artery disease from chest pain type, resting ECG, exercise stress test results, fluoroscopy vessel count, and thallium test result — clinical/invasive test data from a hospital-referral population.

🚬 CDC BRFSS 2015 (lifestyle model)

253,680 respondents to the CDC's Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System survey. Predicts self-reported lifetime history of heart disease or heart attack from smoking, BMI, diet, physical activity, alcohol use, and demographics — general-population survey data with no lab values.

Pooling them would mean pretending a diagnostic test result and a self-reported survey answer, predicting two different outcomes in two different populations, are interchangeable data points. They're modeled separately — reflected in the calculator's two clearly-labeled tabs rather than one blended simulator.

Both models land right in line with published benchmarks for each dataset.

Both land around AUROC ≈ 0.88, matching the widely-cited published benchmark (0.886) for this dataset. Top SHAP drivers: asymptomatic chest pain, ST depression, sex, cholesterol, exercise-induced angina, max heart rate, thallium result, age, and vessels on fluoroscopy — all established cardiac risk factors.

AUROC ≈ 0.85 matches the published benchmark (0.848). Both models correct for the dataset's ~90/10 class imbalance. Ranked by SHAP importance: age, self-rated general health, high blood pressure, sex, and high cholesterol are the strongest predictors — all clinical/demographic. Lifestyle factors (smoking, diet, activity, alcohol, BMI) account for only ~7% of total SHAP-explained importance, with smoking the strongest single lifestyle lever.
